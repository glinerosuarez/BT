# Preferences
- Don't use em dashes.
- Write in a natural, grounded voice.
- Prefer plain statements over salesy framing.
- Show fit through concrete examples, not by announcing fit explicitly.

## Cover Letter
- Do not restate the job description back to the reader.
- Avoid lines like "This experience directly mirrors the role" or "My background aligns well with your requirements."
- Avoid commentary sentences that explain why an example is relevant. Let the example carry the point.
- Avoid cliche transitions such as "This taught me how to..." or "This experience demonstrates my ability to..."
- Prefer specific, matter-of-fact descriptions of what was built, improved, or learned.
- If referencing the company, make it about a real aspect of their product, scale, or environment, not generic enthusiasm.
- Keep the tone restrained. Do not sound like you are trying too hard to fit the role.
- Do not use inflated adjectives such as "complex", "advanced", "cutting-edge", "sophisticated", or similar hype language unless the exact word appears in the source material.
- Do not introduce libraries, frameworks, or tools that are not explicitly present in the source profile.

## Resume Preferences
- Avoid explicit "fit signaling" phrases such as "demonstrating hands-on experience with..." or "gaining experience with..."
- Avoid summary clauses that explain the bullet's importance instead of adding substance.
- Prefer bullets that describe the system, the action, and the result in concrete terms.
- Prefer quantified bullets when the source material supports them.
- Preserve concrete numbers for scale, latency, accuracy, cost, throughput, dataset size, and impact whenever they are available in the source profile.
- If a bullet can be made stronger by using an existing metric from the profile, use the metric instead of a vague qualitative claim.
- Do not invent metrics, percentages, counts, or performance improvements that are not present in the source profile.
- Preserve the meaning of source-backed impact statements exactly when possible. Do not casually rewrite details like UI vs production, or measured accuracy vs estimated quality.
- Do not introduce libraries, frameworks, tools, or methods that are not explicitly present in the source profile.
- Avoid inflated adjectives such as "complex", "advanced", "cutting-edge", "sophisticated", or similar hype language unless the exact word appears in the source material.
- Let scale, complexity, latency, cost, and production context show strength through specifics rather than generic adjectives.
